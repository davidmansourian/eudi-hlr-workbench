01

What this product is

~1 min · Section 1 of 32

The European Digital Identity (EUDI) Wallet is an EU-wide, citizen-controlled, mobile-first electronic identification and attribute-presentation system. It is not a single app — it is an interoperable ecosystem.

Within that ecosystem:

  • A Wallet User uses a Wallet Unit (an instance of a certified Wallet Solution) to receive, store, and present identity and attribute data.
  • The wallet can present Person Identification Data (PID) issued under a Member State's eID scheme at Level of Assurance (LoA) High, plus Qualified and non-qualified Electronic Attestations of Attributes (QEAA / PuB-EAA / EAA).
  • It supports proximity (offline, ISO/IEC 18013-5) and remote (online, OpenID4VP) presentation flows.
  • It supports Qualified Electronic Signatures (QES), pseudonymous authentication, and Strong Customer Authentication for electronic payments.
  • All Member States are required to provide at least one Wallet to citizens within 24 months of the implementing acts entering into force; using the wallet is not mandatory for citizens.

In Article 3(42) of the European Digital Identity Regulation, "European Digital Identity Wallet" is defined as an electronic identification means. Crucially, in ARF terms, the eID means is realised by a Wallet Unit + the PID issued to it + the eID scheme it operates under, taken together. A Wallet Solution alone is not the eID means.

Status of the ARF

The ARF is informative. The only legally binding texts are Regulation (EU) 2024/1183 (the European Digital Identity Regulation) and the adopted Commission Implementing Regulations (CIRs).

Why it exists

~3 min · Regulatory & legal context

The Regulation

The base law is Regulation (EU) 2024/1183, which amends Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 (the original eIDAS Regulation) to establish the European Digital Identity Framework. It mandates Wallet provision, defines the legal effects of attestations, sets requirements for QTSPs and Trust Services, and creates the institutional structure for governance.

The Toolbox & the EDICG

Commission Recommendation (EU) 2021/946 (3 June 2021) created a Common Union Toolbox consisting of:

  • the Architecture and Reference Framework (this body of work),
  • a set of common standards and technical specifications, and
  • a set of common guidelines and best practices.

The European Digital Identity Cooperation Group (EDICG, formerly the eIDAS Expert Group) is the body that drives the work. It exchanges best practice, advises the Commission on draft Implementing/Delegated Acts, supports Supervisory Bodies, and organises peer reviews of eID schemes.

Ecosystem
03

The ecosystem

~5 min · 18 roles & how they relate

The ARF defines 18 distinct roles. A single legal entity can occupy multiple roles, provided it satisfies every applicable requirement and avoids conflicts of interest.

End-user side

User of a Wallet Unit

A natural person in control of the Wallet Unit. The ARF assumes the user device is personal — only the User can access/control the Wallet Unit. PIDs and attestations on the Wallet Unit pertain to that User. The legal-person Wallet topic has been removed from the ARF in favour of a separate business wallet effort.

Issuance side

Wallet Provider

Member State or organisation mandated by one. Makes a certified Wallet Solution available. Responsible for the entire Wallet Unit (including WSCA/WSCD, even if integrated by a third party). Signs and issues WUAs and WIAs.

PID Provider

Verifies user identity at LoA High and issues PIDs. Each Member State has at least one. Need not be a QTSP. Identified via a List of Trusted Entities (LoTE).

QEAA Provider

A QTSP issuing Qualified Electronic Attestations of Attributes. Subject to the full QTSP framework. Identified via a Trusted List.

PuB-EAA Provider

A public-sector body (or designee) responsible for an Authentic Source. Holds a qualified certificate from a QTSP for signing PuB-EAAs. Listed in a Trusted List.

EAA Provider (non-qualified)

Any non-qualified TSP. Supervised under the Regulation but largely governed by sectoral rules.

Authentic Source

Public or private repository recognised by law as the primary source for an attribute (address, age, professional qualification, etc.). A public-sector Authentic Source can also act as a PuB-EAA Provider.

QESRC Provider

A QTSP operating a remote QSCD the Wallet Unit can use to create QES.

Trust infrastructure

Trusted List / LoTE Provider

Maintains and publishes Trusted Lists (for QTSPs) and Lists of Trusted Entities (for non-QTSPs — Wallet Providers, PID Providers, Access CAs, Providers of Registration Certificates). There is no list for Relying Parties; instead each RP gets one or more access certificates.

Access Certificate Authority

Issues access certificates (qualified certificates per ETSI TS 119 411-8) to RPs and to PID/Attestation Providers. Listed in a LoTE. Can issue under a dedicated EUDI scheme or under the eIDAS QWAC scheme.

Provider of Registration Certificates

A QTSP issuing registration certificates — qualified certificates attesting a provider's registration status and scope. Listed in a LoTE.

Registrar

Per Member State, registers PID Providers, QEAA Providers, PuB-EAA Providers, EAA Providers, and Relying Parties. Publishes registry data in machine- and human-readable form (TS5 / TS6 specify the format and API).

Service consumers

Relying Party

Any service provider requesting attributes. Legally also includes Attestation Providers, but the ARF reserves the term for service providers. VLOPs are required to accept the Wallet for user authentication. Must register with their Member State's Registrar, declaring which attributes they intend to request.

Relying Party Instance

The technical software/hardware that interacts with Wallet Units. An RP can run multiple Instances; each gets its own access certificate. The 2026 refinement-round Topic X is pushing toward one access certificate per service.

Intermediary

A Relying Party acting on behalf of intermediated RPs. Legally still an RP. Must register, must not store transaction content, must immediately delete any received attributes after forwarding them.

Oversight & assurance

Conformity Assessment Body (CAB)

Accredited (per Reg 765/2008) by a National Accreditation Body. Certifies Wallet Solutions and audits QTSPs.

National Accreditation Body (NAB)

Per Member State, accredits CABs.

Supervisory Body

Per Member State, supervises Wallet Providers and other ecosystem actors.

Attestation Scheme Provider

Defines a specific attestation type. Publishes a human-readable Rulebook + a machine-readable scheme. The Commission publishes the PID Rulebook; sectoral bodies publish theirs. The Commission operates a Catalogue of Schemes and Rulebooks.

Platform layer

Device Manufacturers & subsystem providers

Provide hardware, OS, secure elements, libraries, app stores. The Wallet Provider is responsible for picking devices/platforms that allow the Wallet Unit to achieve LoA High.

Quick reference: arrows that matter most

For an iOS engineer, the relationships you'll touch most are:

  • PID Provider → Wallet Unit — PID issuance over OpenID4VCI.
  • Attestation Provider → Wallet Unit — attestation issuance over OpenID4VCI.
  • Wallet Unit ↔ Relying Party Instance — presentation over OpenID4VP (remote) or ISO/IEC 18013-5 device retrieval (proximity).
  • Wallet Provider → Wallet Unit — WUA / WIA issuance and lifecycle management.
  • Member State Registrar → Wallet Unit — lookup of RP registration data via TS5 API (when no registration certificate is provided).
  • Authentic Source → QEAA Provider — attribute verification (Topic 42).

04

What the wallet does

~2 min · User-facing functionality

The Wallet is built around four functional clusters.

Identification & authentication

  • Selectively disclose attributes — selective disclosure is mandatory in both formats.
  • Securely authenticate the Relying Party before disclosure (the Wallet must verify the RP, not just trust it).
  • Onboard with PID/Attestation Providers using existing eID schemes.
  • Inform the user whether the RP is registered for the requested attributes; warn if not.
  • Provide a dashboard for transaction history with: review past interactions, request data erasure (GDPR Art. 17), report suspicious RPs to the Data Protection Authority.

Attribute exchange

  • Request, store, present PIDs / attestations under the User's sole control.
  • Backup attributes / configurations (Topic 34 — Migration Object).
  • Prevent RP linkability through the issuance methods discussed later.

Qualified Electronic Signatures

  • Available by default and free of charge within the Wallet for non-professional purposes — a hard regulatory requirement.
  • Backed by either a local QSCD, an external QSCD, or a remote QSCD managed by a QTSP (QESRC Provider).
  • Must produce PAdES-baseline signatures at minimum; SHOULD support XAdES, JAdES, CAdES, ASiC.

Pseudonyms

  • Generate, store (encrypted, locally), and present pseudonyms.
  • Pseudonyms must be specific and unique per RP (CIR 2024/2979 Art 14).
  • RPs cannot refuse pseudonyms when identification is not legally required.
  • Four use cases — only (A) is fully required by current CIR; B partial; C/D need additional spec work.

Use cases manual

The ARF mentions a forthcoming Use Cases Manual distributed alongside the ARF, with standardised blueprints for: online identification, QES, mobile Driving Licence, payment SCA, representation paradigm, and others (health data, educational credentials, Digital Travel Credentials, CBDC, social security incl. PDA1 and EHIC).

Architecture
05

Reference architecture

~3 min · Components & interfaces

The Wallet Unit (composite)

A Wallet Unit is a composite — not just an app. It comprises:

  1. Wallet Instance (WI) — the application installed/configured on the user's device. Handles UI, presentation flows, OpenID4VP/VCI orchestration, transaction logging, dashboard, data subject rights features, and communication with the WSCA/WSCD or keystore.
  2. Wallet Secure Cryptographic Device (WSCD) — a tamper-resistant device that protects critical assets (PID private keys, WUA private keys) and provides cryptographic operations.
  3. Wallet Secure Cryptographic Application (WSCA) — the application linked to the WSCD that manages critical assets via the WSCD's primitives.
  4. Keystore (distinct concept since recent revisions) — a hardware-backed repository for non-critical cryptographic assets (keys for attestations not requiring LoA High). Examples: Secure Element, TPM, TEE, secure enclave, or remote HSM.
  5. Storage component — encrypted local storage for PIDs, attestations, configuration, and transaction logs.

A Wallet Solution is the combination of software, hardware, services, settings, and configurations a Wallet Provider makes available; a Wallet Unit is one configured instance of that Solution issued to one User.

⚑ Critical asset rule (memorise this)

Anything whose compromise (availability, confidentiality, integrity) would have a very serious, debilitating effect on trust in the Wallet Unit is a critical asset and must live in a WSCA/WSCD. Examples: private/secret keys for the PID, for WUAs, and for attestations whose level of security is High. Other keys may live in a keystore instead. (Annex 1 + WIAM_14 family.)

Five design principles

  1. User-centricity — sole user control, clear UX, options to revoke or request deletion.
  2. Accessibility — must comply with EN 301 549, EAA, etc.
  3. Interoperability — standardised data formats, protocols, machine-readable schemes, cross-border recognition.
  4. Privacy by design — selective disclosure, holder + device binding, pseudonyms, unlinkability, minimisation, embedded disclosure policies, transaction logs accessible only to the user.
  5. Security by design — layered controls; cryptographic protection through the lifecycle; certification of Wallet Solutions; incident notification (CIR 2025/847).

Interfaces a Wallet Unit must implement

  • Issuance interface (HTTPS to PID/Attestation Providers): mutual auth, OpenID4VCI, supports initial issuance and re-issuance.
  • Proximity presentation interface: ISO/IEC 18013-5 over NFC, BLE, or Wi-Fi; mutual authentication; selective disclosure; cryptographic device + holder binding proofs.
  • Remote presentation interface: OpenID4VP per HAIP profile; same-device (HTTP redirect, Android Intent, iOS URL scheme, deep link) and cross-device (QR code with one-time-use token over a secure back-channel); RP authenticated via access certificate; encrypted response (OIA_09).
  • Revocation checking: status-list / CRL / OCSP-style mechanisms; supports both online and offline checking; fallback when revocation services unavailable.
  • User authentication interface: multi-factor, possibly biometric, OS PIN, or Wallet-Unit-specific PIN; recovery mechanisms.

WSCA · WSCD · Keystore

~3 min · The cryptographic substrate

The five WSCD architectures

The ARF (Section 4.5) explicitly enumerates five WSCD architecture types, plus hybrids:

TypeWhere keys liveNetwork?Threat-model fit
Remote WSCDWallet Provider's HSM-backed serverYesHigh-risk personal devices; institutional deployments
Local External WSCDDetachable hardware (USB key, smartcard, hardware wallet)NoHigh-security; shared/borrowed devices
Local Internal WSCDTEE / Secure Enclave / dedicated co-processor on deviceNoGeneral consumer mobile case
Local Native WSCDOS-managed key storage; no dedicated HW isolationNoTrusted environments; convenience-first
HybridCombinations of the aboveMixedMost realistic deployments

Engineering implications

  • The ARF is architecture-neutral. An iOS Wallet using Secure Enclave is a Local Internal WSCD; one using Apple Smart Card kit + a YubiKey is Local External; one using an MDM-managed remote HSM is Remote.
  • Whichever you pick, the Wallet Solution as a whole must be certified at LoA High. Individual components may be certified lower if duly justified (CIR 2024/2981 + CIR 2015/1502).
  • The Wallet Provider is responsible for the WSCD even if Apple/Google supplied it. Other ecosystem actors don't need to interact with or trust the WSCD vendor directly.

Keystore vs. WSCA/WSCD

Critical assets → WSCA/WSCD. Non-critical → keystore (hardware-backed). Most attestations issued for sensitive use cases (PID, WUA, signatures, payment SCA) require WSCA/WSCD; lower-assurance attestations (event tickets, vouchers, etc.) may live in a keystore.

WUA, WIA, and KA

  • WUA — historically a single attestation by the Wallet Provider that the Wallet Unit and its components conform to requirements and that listed public keys belong to the Wallet Unit.
  • The 2026 Topic C refinement is splitting this:
    • WIA = attestation to the integrity/authenticity of the Wallet Instance (the app), used during all PID/attestation issuance.
    • KA (Key Attestation) = attestation to the WSCA/WSCD or keystore properties for the keys involved, used only for PID and device-bound attestations.
    • "WUA" becomes the umbrella term.
  • Revocation semantics differ: WIA revocation means the Wallet Instance is revoked; KA revocation means the WSCD/keystore is revoked (either type-shared across all units of that WSCD type, or per-instance).
  • Revocation maintenance period is a new concept being introduced: WUAs themselves are short-lived (TS3 caps at ~24h), so a maintenance period is communicated separately to bound how long a Wallet Provider commits to maintaining revocation status. PID expiry must not exceed this maintenance period.

Cryptographic algorithms

All cryptographic operations across the ecosystem must use algorithms from the ENISA ECCG Agreed Cryptographic Mechanisms v2.0. Hardware-level cryptography also references ENISA EUCC Cryptographic Guidelines v2.

Data model

~6 min · Attestations, formats, rulebooks

Attestation elements

Every attestation has: issuer info, subject identification, attribute data, validity period, proof mechanism, metadata.

Categories

  • PID — LoA High, device-bound, Member-State-issued, eID-scheme operated.
  • QEAA — qualified, by QTSP, full eIDAS trust framework.
  • PuB-EAA — issued by/on behalf of a public-sector Authentic Source; signed under a QTSP-issued qualified certificate.
  • EAA — non-qualified, sectoral.

⚑ Rules of thumb
  • A PID must be issued in both mdoc and SD-JWT VC (PID_02).
  • An mDL must be mdoc-only and must not be SD-JWT VC.
  • A QEAA / PuB-EAA scheme must specify mdoc, SD-JWT VC, or both (ARB_01). Non-qualified EAAs may also use W3C VCDM 2.0 (ARB_01a).
  • Any attestation usable in proximity must be mdoc (ARB_02), because OpenID4VP requires internet.

PID Rulebook (Annex 3.01)

  • mdoc attestation type: eu.europa.ec.eudi.pid.1. Namespace identifier identical.
  • SD-JWT VC base type: urn:eudi:pid:1.
  • Mandatory attributes (CIR 2024/2977): family_name, given_name, birth_date, birth_place, nationality.
  • Optional: resident_address, resident_country, resident_state, resident_city, resident_postal_code, resident_street, resident_house_number, personal_administrative_number, portrait, family_name_birth, given_name_birth, sex, email_address, mobile_phone_number.
  • Mandatory metadata: expiry_date, issuing_authority, issuing_country. Optional: document_number, issuing_jurisdiction, location_status.
  • Additional optional rulebook attributes: issuance_date, trust_anchor, attestation_legal_category (= "PID").
  • Portrait: single JPEG, ISO/IEC 19794-5 quality (Full Frontal Image) but without the format headers/blocks. JPEG only.
  • CBOR encoding: minimum-length integers/strings/arrays/maps, no indefinite-length items, no fractional seconds, UTC offset only ("Z"), tstr ≤ 150 chars, full Unicode.
  • Administrative vs. technical validity: administrative is the long PID validity (years); technical is the per-token validity (days/weeks). PID Providers re-issue technically-fresh PIDs throughout the administrative period to limit linkability.
  • For SD-JWT VC: standard JWT nbf / exp for technical validity; nested properties supported (address.street_address, etc.) for granular selective disclosure.
  • Domestic namespaces: PID Providers may extend with a domestic namespace, e.g. eu.europa.ec.eudi.pid.de.1. Must be published in a Rulebook compliant with Topic 12.
  • Selective disclosure of nationality in mdoc: the entire array is disclosed together; SD-JWT VC permits selective disclosure of individual nationalities.

mDL Rulebook (Annex 3.02)

  • Fully delegated to ISO/IEC 18013-5; no EUDI-specific changes.
  • mDL must be mdoc-only — not SD-JWT VC.
  • Detailed attribute schema lives in the eudi-doc-attestation-rulebooks-catalog repo (the page in the ARF site is a redirect notice).

Attestation Rulebooks & Schemes

For any attestation type:

  • The Scheme Provider publishes a Rulebook (human-readable) and a scheme (machine-readable).
  • Each format gets a specified attribute encoding (CBOR for mdoc, JSON for SD-JWT VC).
  • For mdoc: each attribute lives in an attribute namespace with a globally unique identifier; each attribute identifier is unique within the namespace.
  • For SD-JWT VC: claim names must be either an IANA-registered JWT claim, an RFC 7519 Public Name, or a Private Name specific to the type.
  • Mandatory / optional / conditional presence specified per attribute.
  • For QEAA / PuB-EAA: Rulebook must include the attestation_legal_category attribute (ARB_25), the cryptographically_bound_to attribute when applicable (ARB_28), and use the Attestation Rulebook Template in the catalog repo (ARB_29).
  • For SD-JWT VC, every claim must have its selective-disclosability declared (ARB_30); a Type Metadata Document is recommended (ARB_31).
  • Each Rulebook also declares whether the attestation is device-bound (ARB_34).

Catalogues

  • Catalogue of Attributes — standardised attribute identifiers, semantics, types, encoding, multilingual labels (Topic 25).
  • Catalogue of Attestation Schemes — the canonical registry of available attestation types, their Rulebook URLs, scheme URLs, version info, applicability, attributes.
  • The Commission operates the catalogue infrastructure; CIR 2025/1569 Article 8 governs it; Technical Specification TS11 specifies interfaces and formats.

Protocols
08

Issuance protocol

~2 min · OpenID4VCI + the four methods

Protocol

Base protocol: OpenID for Verifiable Credential Issuance (OpenID4VCI), profiled per the OpenID4VC High Assurance Interoperability Profile (HAIP).

Required steps (Section 6.6.2)

  1. Wallet Unit authenticates the Provider (cert chain to LoTE/Trusted-List trust anchor; non-revoked; non-expired).
  2. Provider validates the Wallet Unit:
    • Verifies WUA signature against Wallet Provider trust anchor in LoTE.
    • Optionally validates WSCA/WSCD or keystore properties (e.g. Common Criteria level).
    • Verifies that the new key is protected by the WSCD or keystore.
  3. Provider verifies WUA is not revoked.
  4. Wallet Unit verifies the issued PID/attestation: signature, listing of provider, validity period, format.
  5. For PIDs specifically, the user must explicitly activate the PID before it can be presented.
  6. Optionally provisioning of an embedded disclosure policy.
  7. Optionally batch issuance for efficiency (one Wallet Unit verification, multiple attestations issued).

The four issuance methods (Topic 10)

The single most important privacy-engineering decision in the protocol.

MethodNameWhat it doesMandatory?
AOnce-only attestationsIssued in batches; each used exactly once, then discardedMandatory for WUAs; mandatory minimum (A or B) for all PIDs/attestations
BLimited-timeValid for a short period; multiple uses; periodic re-issuanceMandatory minimum (A or B)
CRotating-batchIssued in batches; used in random order; once all used, batch resetsOptional
DPer-Relying PartyWallet issues a different attestation per RP; same RP gets the same oneOptional

Method A maximally mitigates RP linkability; Method B is operationally simpler.

Privacy-critical issuance HLRs

  • Unique elements (salts, hashes, identifiers, batch timestamps, public keys) must have negligible collision probability.
  • Batch timestamps must not be usable for user correlation.
  • Unique elements must be discarded post-issuance by the RP and Wallet Unit.

Re-issuance triggers

  • Limit RP linkability (issue multiple variants with different keys/SD-JWT variants).
  • Attribute value change (address change, qualification update).
  • Synchronous re-issuance during migration to a new Wallet Unit.

Protocols
09

Presentation protocols

~3 min · Proximity & remote

Proximity (ISO/IEC 18013-5)

  • Used for in-person verification (police, border, age check, mDL).
  • Channels: NFC, BLE, Wi-Fi (or QR-code-driven engagement).
  • Steps: device engagement → request transmission (attribute list, optional transactional data) → user authentication → user approval → response (selectively disclosed attributes + cryptographic proofs of device + holder binding) → RP verification.
  • No internet required (so no real-time tracking via network logs).
  • The Wallet must not support ISO/IEC 18013-5 server retrieval (ProxId_02). Only device retrieval is permitted.

Remote (OpenID4VP)

  • Profiled per HAIP.
  • For mdoc requests over redirects: comply with HAIP §5, §5.1, §5.3.1 + the ISO mdocs profile in HAIP §6.
  • For SD-JWT VC: HAIP §5, §5.1, §5.3.2 + the IETF SD-JWT VCs profile in HAIP §6.
  • Same-device flow: HTTP redirect, Android Intent, iOS URL scheme, deep link.
  • Cross-device flow: QR code with one-time-use token; back-channel communication.
  • The presentation response must be encrypted so only the RP Instance can read it (OIA_09).
  • The Wallet Unit verifies the RP's access certificate (chained to a notified Access CA trust anchor), checks attribute-scope match against the registration certificate or the Registrar's online API, evaluates any embedded disclosure policy, then asks the user for approval.

W3C Digital Credentials API

The ARF requires support conditionally:

  • The API must be a W3C Recommendation.
  • It must satisfy the expectations in the Topic F discussion paper.
  • It must be broadly supported across browsers and OSes.

If supported, by default the Wallet must only disclose attestation types to the framework — not the existence of attributes or their values. The user must have a global setting to disable disclosure.

Multiple-attestation choice rules

If multiple PIDs of the same encoding exist on the wallet and the RP requests "a PID", the Wallet must ask the user which to present (OIA_10). Same for attestations of the same encoding + same type (OIA_11).

Privacy hygiene at the RP (OIA_16)

The RP Instance must discard the values of all unique elements (salts, hashes, public keys, signatures, timestamps) as soon as they're no longer needed and must not transmit them to the RP backend or anyone else.

Trust-throughout-lifecycle pattern

For each entity, Chapter 6 walks through registration → notification → certificate issuance → trust-anchor inclusion → operation → suspension/revocation. The pattern repeats and is the single most useful mental model when reading the ARF.

Trust steps in a presentation

When an RP receives a presented attestation (Section 6.6.3):

  1. Authenticate the attestation: signature + issuer trust anchor + listing in trust list + non-revocation + non-expiration + format.
  2. Verify the attestation is not revoked (status list / CRL / OCSP).
  3. Verify device binding: WSCD-bound key + possession proof.
  4. Verify holder binding: either RP performs its own (KYC, biometric match) or RP trusts the issuer to have done so during issuance.
  5. Verify combined presentation of attributes (if multiple): same WSCD via shared key; or issuer-embedded cryptographic commitment (cryptographically_bound_to); or selective-disclosure proof.
  6. Trust the issuer to have authenticated the Wallet Unit and Wallet Provider.
  7. Optionally trust the issuer to keep verifying revocation throughout lifetime.

Reporting

The Wallet must allow the user to report a suspicious request to the supervising Data Protection Authority (Topic 50, RPT_DPA_*). It must also allow the user to request data erasure from any RP (Topic 48, DATA_DLT_*).

Wallet Unit lifecycle

~4 min · Install → activate → manage → revoke

Phases

  1. Installation of the Wallet Instance on a User device.
  2. Activation (registration with the Wallet Provider, key generation, WUA/WIA issuance).
  3. Management (issuance of PIDs/attestations, presentation, re-issuance, updates).
  4. Revocation.
  5. Uninstallation (with secure deletion of cryptographic assets).

Installation requirements

  • Distribution preferred via official OS app store (WIAM_01). Other channels permitted only with explicit user instructions on bypassing OS sideloading restrictions and on verifying authenticity (WIAM_02).
  • The user must verify Wallet Solution authenticity via cryptographic signature.
  • The user must verify the Wallet Solution is certified for the PID scheme they plan to use, and the device meets security requirements.

Activation flow (Topic 40)

The Wallet Provider:

  1. Verifies the Wallet Instance is genuine (WIAM_04).
  2. Collects necessary device + WSCA/WSCD + keystore data (WIAM_05). Three categories:
    • Required for issuing WUA/WIA.
    • Optional for managing the Wallet Unit.
    • Fraud / risk signal monitoring — security-posture data only, no behavioural data, no geolocation.
  3. Establishes two user authentication mechanisms:
    • OS-level authentication (mandatory for all operations).
    • WSCD-level authentication for cryptographic operations involving critical assets at LoA High.
    • A Wallet-Unit-specific PIN may be additionally enforced (WIAM_15b).
  4. Issues at least one WUA per WSCA/WSCD plus one WUA per keystore (WIAM_10), and verifies the corresponding private keys are protected.
  5. Sets up a user account at the Wallet Provider, usable for revocation requests in case of theft/loss. Authentication methods independent of the device. The user may register an alias (WIAM_06).
  6. Issues a means for the user to verify the Wallet Solution's certification status (WIAM_10a).

Communication between Wallet Provider and Wallet Instance

Mutually authenticated; should be encrypted (WIAM_12).

⚠ Privacy guard against the Wallet Provider

WIAM_12a requires the Wallet Unit to ensure that the Wallet Provider cannot access: which attestations are present; their status; attribute values; or contents of the transaction log.

The Wallet Provider runs the infrastructure but is not allowed to surveil what the user does with the wallet.

Uninstallation

On uninstall, the Wallet Instance requests the WSCA/WSCD and keystore(s) to delete all cryptographic assets related to the Wallet Unit and its PIDs/device-bound attestations (if connected). Where the WSCD is offline (e.g. external smartcard not present), the Provider's policy decides how to handle eventual cleanup. If the Wallet supports the W3C Digital Credentials API, it must inform the framework that no PIDs/attestations are stored.

Strong User Authentication

~2 min · Electronic payments & SCA

Regulatory frame

PSD2 + Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2018/389 (RTS for SCA) + Regulation Article 5f(2) (uses the term "strong user authentication"). The wallet must comply with SCA when accessing a payment account online or initiating an electronic payment, and any remote action carrying payment-fraud risk.

The SUA attestation (Topic 20)

A Strong User Authentication (SUA) attestation is a dedicated, device-bound attestation issued to the Wallet Unit that allows the Wallet to satisfy SCA. The presentation request for an SUA attestation includes transactional data (e.g. payment amount, payee). The Wallet:

  1. Validates the transactional data is intended for the given attestation and conforms to the technical spec/Rulebook (SUA_07).
  2. Renders the data in a clear, WYSIWYS manner (SUA_06 + Topic AA dialogue requirements).
  3. Authenticates the user and obtains explicit approval.
  4. Includes (a representation of) the transactional data in the device-binding signature (using SD-JWT VC key binding or ISO/IEC 18013-5 mdoc authentication) — SUA_05.

The resulting signature value is the authentication code required by PSD2.

Two flow models (Topic AA)

  • Issuer-requested (2-party): ASPSP is both issuer and RP; supports all SCA use cases.
  • Payee-requested (3-party): separate issuer and RP; only payment-initiation SCA permitted.

Lifecycle

  • Registration: identify+authenticate user (e.g. via PID), issue an SUA attestation linking the Wallet Unit to a service/account.
  • SCA: every transaction signs the per-transaction data.
  • De-registration: revoke or delete the SUA attestation.

Open question

Whether native SD-JWT and mdoc mechanisms are sufficient for the authentication code, or whether additional mechanisms (e.g. JTI-based codes) are needed (Topic AA Q3).

No ZKP scheme is currently selected for the EUDI Wallet ecosystem. ZKP is the only known full mitigation for AP linkability when using salted-hash attestations.

Topic 53's HLRs (ZKP_01–ZKP_09) define what a viable scheme must do:

  • Uses only ECCG v2 algorithms.
  • Does not undermine LoA High user authentication.

The Commission is exploring ZKP-based age verification in particular.

Two-phase plan

User auth & WIAM

~2 min · The most operationally critical HLRs

The WIAM_* HLRs in Topic 40 are the most operationally important set for an app developer.

Key items

  • WIAM_14 — A WSCA/WSCD managing PID critical assets must authenticate the user at LoA High before any cryptographic operation. (LoA High requires multi-factor auth meeting CIR 2015/1502 §2.2.1.)
  • WIAM_14a — Same applies for WUA critical assets.
  • WIAM_14b — For attestations of "level of security High", WSCD must auth user at the corresponding level. (The ARF uses "level of security" because LoA strictly applies only to eID means.)
  • WIAM_14c — Non-critical cryptographic assets may live in a keystore.
  • WIAM_15 — Wallet Instance must use multi-factor auth via the device (one factor is device possession).
  • WIAM_15a — Wallet Instance must enforce OS-level user authentication with adequate security policies for any operation that does not involve PIDs/WUAs/high-security attestations.
  • WIAM_15b — A Wallet-Unit-specific PIN may supplement OS auth; the Wallet Provider can mandate it for all users or allow per-user choice.
  • WIAM_16 — Wallet Instance forces the device to enable session timeout / re-auth interval.
  • WIAM_17 — WSCA/WSCD must be able to authenticate at LoA High per CIR 2015/1502 §2.2.1.
  • WIAM_19 — WSCA/WSCD and keystore must be able to prove possession of a private key on Wallet Instance request (e.g. by signing a challenge).
  • WIAM_20 — WSCA/WSCD must protect a private key for its entire lifetime; key extraction in cleartext is forbidden; any encrypted export must preserve the in-WSCA protection level.
  • WIAM_21 — Whenever the WSCA/WSCD authenticates the user, the Wallet Unit SHOULD opportunistically clean up unused PID/attestation cryptographic assets (so the user isn't burdened with separate cleanup auth events).

Practical UX implication

A single user authentication event is allowed to cover all cryptographic operations required to complete a single "Wallet Unit action". The Wallet Provider must define what constitutes a "Wallet Unit action", balancing UX vs security; certification verifies adequacy.

Dashboard, logs & rights

~3 min · DASH_* + GDPR Art. 17 + DPA reporting

Logging requirements

  • Log all transactions: issuance, presentation, W2W, pseudonym registration/presentation, signature creation, data-deletion requests, DPA reports, PID/attestation deletions (DASH_02).
  • Retain at least the legal minimum retention period; warn user before any deletion forced by size limits (DASH_02a).
  • Display all transactions through a UI overview (DASH_02b).
  • Per TS10 — confidentiality/integrity/authenticity protections (DASH_02c).

What's logged per transaction

  • Presentation: timestamp, RP name+id+Member State, intermediary info, attestation types and attribute IDs requested vs. presented, failure reason, RP Registrar URL, RP data-deletion contact (URL/email/phone), supervising DPA contact, intended use + privacy policy URL.
  • Presentation transaction must NOT log attribute values (DASH_03a) — log attribute identifiers only.
  • W2W: timestamp, role (Holder/Verifier), attestation types and attribute IDs requested/presented, failure reason. Same — no values.
  • Pseudonym: timestamp, RP info if known, registration-vs-presentation, failure reason.
  • Transactional data (SUA): logged only to the extent required by the applicable Technical Specification (TS12 for PSD2 SCA: payment transaction identifier + merchant name).
  • Signature: timestamp, document/data signed (if available), failure reason.
  • Issuance: timestamp, Provider info, attestation type requested/issued, batch size, failure reason, whether triggered by user or by Wallet Unit, Registrar's online service URL.
  • Deletion: timestamp, attestation type, Provider info.

User capabilities

  • Delete any transaction from the log (DASH_06a) — with consequence warning even if before legal retention end.
  • No other entity may delete transactions (DASH_06b).
  • Export transaction details to a user-chosen external/remote storage location (DASH_07).

  • Wallet UI must show the EUDI Wallet Trust Mark, certification info, and links to certification status (DASH_09 + TS1).
  • Positioning follows Commission design guidelines (DASH_09a).

Data deletion requests (Topic 48)

The Wallet supports user-initiated GDPR Art. 17 erasure requests via three channels (URL in external browser, draft email, phone call) per DATA_DLT_02. Requests log to the dashboard. RPs must authenticate users before executing requests (DATA_DLT_07). All parties must comply with TS7.

DPA reporting (Topic 50)

The Wallet enables users to report suspicious presentation requests to the supervising DPA via the same three channels. If supervising-DPA contacts aren't in the log, fall back to the local DPA. Substantiating data may be attached in machine-readable form per TS10. TS8 governs the format.

Cross-cutting tradeoffs

~3 min · Decision points to internalise

These are the recurring decision-points where the ARF balances competing concerns. Internalising these will save you hours of re-reading.

Privacy vs. operational complexity

Issuance Method A maximises privacy but forces re-issuance per use (more network calls, more user friction, more Provider load). Method B minimises ops cost but partially leaks linkability. Picking A vs B per-attestation type is a real product decision.

User control vs. user friction

Every single attribute disclosure requires explicit user approval. Selective disclosure is mandatory. The dashboard offers per-transaction logs and one-tap data-deletion / DPA-reporting flows. Non-negotiable but materially affects UX. Build for both clarity and speed.

Wallet Provider visibility vs. user privacy

The Wallet Provider must monitor security posture (Topic T) but must not see the contents of the Wallet Unit (WIAM_12a). The data-collection list in WPSM_01 is a permissive ceiling, not a floor; minimise within it.

Centralised certification vs. composite certification

The Wallet Solution as a whole is certified at LoA High, but its components (TEE, Secure Enclave, OS) are typically certified separately under different schemes. The composite-certification dependency analysis is real engineering work.

mdoc vs. SD-JWT VC

mdoc dominates proximity (offline). SD-JWT VC dominates remote (web). Most attestations need both. Selective-disclosure semantics differ:

  • mdoc: every data element is selectively disclosable; key-value pairs inside a data element are not.
  • SD-JWT VC: every claim, nested property, and array entry can be configured per Rulebook (ARB_30).

Local vs. remote QSCD

Local: better UX (no network for signing), harder to deploy at scale, dependency on device hardware. Remote: easier provisioning, networked dependency, requires SCAL 2 + ETSI 431-1/2 + 432.

Same-device vs. cross-device remote presentation

Same-device is the expected default UX on mobile. Cross-device (QR) is required for desktop and shared devices. The QR flow needs careful one-time-token handling and back-channel security to prevent session hijacking.

Trust delegation in presentation

The ARF lets RPs delegate user binding to issuers (Section 6.6.3.9 — "Trust Approach"). This is operationally vital — it means an RP doesn't need to redo KYC for every presentation — but means the issuer's authentication processes carry a lot of weight.

Intermediaries

Intermediaries are first-class but tightly constrained: must register, must not store transaction content, must immediately delete attributes after forwarding (RPI_10), must be visible in the Wallet UI alongside the intermediated RP (RPI_07). Builders of intermediated services need to plan for this disclosure UX.

Embedded disclosure policies vs. RP scope checks

These are complementary:

  • RP scope check: Wallet enforces against the Registrar's record of what attributes the RP registered to receive.
  • Embedded disclosure policy: Issuer-controlled, attestation-specific, RP-allowlisting or root-of-trust constraint.

Both must be evaluated; both must be communicated to the user.
